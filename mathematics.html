DERIVATION 1
Derivation 1: Einstein-Cartan-Holst Action to Parity-Odd Effective Action \(S_{\rm eff} = \int d^4x\,\sqrt{-g}\;\frac{\alpha}{M}\;\varepsilon^{abcd}\,K_{ab}\,R_{cd}\)

Starting Point: The Einstein-Cartan-Holst Action

ECH Action. The gravitational action in Einstein-Cartan theory with the Holst term is:

\[S_{\rm ECH} = \frac{1}{16\pi G} \int d^4x \, e \left[R + \frac{1}{\gamma} \varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} + \frac{1}{4} T^{abc} T_{abc}\right] + S_{\rm matter}\]

Here \(e = \det(e^a{}_\mu)\) is the tetrad determinant, \(R\) is the Ricci scalar constructed from the full (torsionful) connection, \(T^{abc}\) is the torsion tensor, and \(\gamma\) is the Barbero-Immirzi parameter from loop quantum gravity. The value \(\gamma = 0.274 \pm 0.020\) is fixed by black hole entropy counting (Meissner 2004, Domagala-Lewandowski 2004).

Index convention: The Holst term is written here as \(\varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\) (spacetime indices), which abbreviates the first-order expression \(e^I_\mu e^J_\nu\,\varepsilon_{IJKL}\,R^{KL}_{\rho\sigma}\) (with \(I,J,K,L\) internal Lorentz indices). In the absence of torsion, this is topological (it reduces to the Nieh-Yan invariant plus boundary terms). However, when torsion is present, it contributes non-trivially to the equations of motion and introduces parity violation.

Torsion Activation by Fermions

Torsion sourced by spin density. In Einstein-Cartan theory, torsion is not a propagating degree of freedom — it is algebraically determined by the spin density of matter. When fermions couple to gravity via the minimal prescription \(D_\mu \psi = (\partial_\mu + \frac{1}{4}\omega_\mu^{ab}\gamma_{ab})\psi\), the torsion equation of motion gives:

\[T^{abc} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}\, S^{abc}\]

where the spin density tensor is:

\[S^{abc} = \frac{1}{4}\, \bar{\psi}\, \gamma^{[a}\gamma^{bc]}\, \psi\]

The antisymmetry of the spin tensor \(S^{abc} = S^{[abc]}\) means torsion is completely antisymmetric (axial). This is a key property: the contorsion tensor \(K^{abc} = \frac{1}{2}(T^{abc} - T^{bac} + T^{cab})\) inherits this antisymmetry and can be expressed in terms of a single axial vector.

Integrating Out Torsion

Effective four-fermion interaction. Since torsion is algebraic (non-propagating) in EC theory, we can substitute the torsion equation of motion back into the action. This yields an effective four-fermion contact interaction:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\rm int} = -\frac{3\pi G_N}{2} \times \frac{\gamma^2}{\gamma^2 + 1} \times J^{\mu}_{(A)}\, J_{(A)\mu}\]

where \(J^{\mu}_{(A)} = \bar{\psi}\gamma^{\mu}\gamma^5\psi\) is the axial fermion current.

The factor \(\gamma^2/(\gamma^2 + 1)\) encodes the Barbero-Immirzi parameter dependence. For \(\gamma = 0.274\): \(\gamma^2/(\gamma^2 + 1) \approx 0.070\). This interaction is suppressed by \(G_N \sim M_{\rm Pl}^{-2}\) and is therefore negligible at low energies, but becomes significant near the Planck scale.

Critically, the axial-axial structure \(J_{(A)}^\mu J_{(A)\mu}\) violates parity. This is the microscopic origin of all parity-odd effects in the BigBounce framework.

Parity-Odd Effective Action

From the Holst 4-form. The parity-odd piece of the ECH action is the Holst term, which in first-order variables (tetrad \(e^I\), connection \(\omega^{IJ}\)) reads:

\[S_{\rm Holst} = \frac{M_{\rm Pl}^2}{\gamma}\int e_I\wedge e_J\wedge R^{IJ}(\omega)\]

This is a manifestly diffeomorphism-invariant 4-form (two tetrad 1-forms \(\wedge\) one curvature 2-form). When the connection is decomposed as \(\omega^{IJ} = \mathring{\omega}^{IJ} + K^{IJ}\) (Levi-Civita + contorsion), the Holst term generates contorsion-curvature cross terms. After integrating out torsion and including quantum corrections, the effective parity-odd action becomes:

\[S_{\rm eff} = \frac{\alpha}{M}\int e_I\wedge e_J\wedge \mathcal{F}^{IJ}[K,\mathring{R}]\]

where \(\mathcal{F}^{IJ} = \mathring{D}K^{IJ} + K^I_{\ K}\wedge K^{KJ}\) collects the contorsion-dependent pieces, \(M \sim \sqrt{\gamma}\,M_{\rm Pl}\) is the area-gap mass scale, and \(\alpha\) is a dimensionless coupling (\([\alpha] = M^0\)). In component notation, the leading contribution reduces to \(\int d^4x\,\sqrt{-g}\,(\alpha/M)\,\varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}\,e^I_\mu e^J_\nu\,\mathcal{F}_{IJ\rho\sigma}\), a scalar density of mass dimension 4.

Notational shorthand: We sometimes write the component integrand as “\(\varepsilon^{abcd}K_{ab}R_{cd}\)” (internal Lorentz indices) — this suppresses the tetrad factors that make the expression a proper 4-form. All variational calculations use the complete first-order expression above.

Dimensional Verification

Dimensional check. Working in natural units where \([\text{mass}] = [\text{length}]^{-1} = [\text{time}]^{-1}\):

\([K_{ab}] = [\text{mass}]^1\) — extrinsic curvature has dimensions of inverse length.

\([R_{cd}] = [\text{mass}]^2\) — Riemann curvature has dimensions of inverse length squared.

The operator \(\varepsilon^{abcd} K_{ab} R_{cd}\) therefore has mass dimension 3.

For the action integrand \(\sqrt{-g} \times (\alpha/M) \times \varepsilon^{abcd} K_{ab} R_{cd}\) to have mass dimension 4 (required for the Lagrangian density in 4D), the coefficient must carry one power of mass in the denominator: \([\alpha/M] = [\text{mass}]^{-1}\). With \(\alpha\) dimensionless, \(M\) is a mass scale — naturally \(M \sim M_{\rm Pl}\).

DERIVATION 2
Derivation 2: Modified Friedmann Equations and Quantum Bounce \(H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho\left[1-\frac{\rho}{\rho_{\rm crit}}\right]\)

Torsion-Modified Friedmann Equation

Spin-fluid cosmology. In Einstein-Cartan theory with a spin fluid (matter whose intrinsic spin density is non-negligible), the Friedmann equation acquires a quadratic correction in the energy density. Starting from the EC field equations with the Weyssenhoff spin fluid ansatz and assuming FLRW symmetry:

\[H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho\left[1 - \frac{\rho}{\rho_{\rm crit}}\right]\]

This is structurally identical to the effective Friedmann equation in loop quantum cosmology (LQC), but here it arises from the classical EC framework with quantum corrections encoded in the spin-torsion coupling. The key point: the \(-\rho^2/\rho_{\rm crit}\) term provides a repulsive contribution that dominates at high densities.

Critical Density from the Barbero-Immirzi Parameter

Critical density. The critical density at which the bounce occurs is determined entirely by the Barbero-Immirzi parameter:

\[\rho_{\rm crit} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{32\pi^2\gamma^3}\,\rho_{\rm Pl} \simeq 0.27\,\rho_{\rm Pl}\]

where \(\rho_{\rm Pl} = c^5/(\hbar G^2) \approx 5.16 \times 10^{93}\;\text{g/cm}^3\) is the Planck density.

Let us verify the numerical coefficient. With \(\gamma = 0.274\):

\(\gamma^3 = 0.274^3 = 0.02057\)

\(32\pi^2 \times 0.02057 = 32 \times 9.8696 \times 0.02057 = 6.495\)

\(\sqrt{3}/6.495 = 1.732/6.495 = 0.267 \approx 0.27\)   VERIFIED

This result is remarkable: the bounce density is sub-Planckian (\(0.27\,\rho_{\rm Pl}\)), which means the semiclassical treatment remains self-consistent. We never need to invoke full quantum gravity — the bounce occurs in a regime where the effective field theory is still valid.

Bounce Condition

H = 0 at the bounce. The modified Friedmann equation has two roots for \(H^2 = 0\):

(i) \(\rho = 0\) — the trivial vacuum solution

(ii) \(\rho = \rho_{\rm crit}\) — the bounce point

At the bounce, the Hubble parameter vanishes and the scale factor reaches a minimum. The acceleration equation (second Friedmann equation) gives:

\[\frac{\ddot{a}}{a}\bigg|_{\rm bounce} = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho_{\rm crit}\left[\frac{2\rho_{\rm crit}}{\rho_{\rm crit}} - 1\right] = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\,\rho_{\rm crit} > 0\]

The positive acceleration at the bounce confirms this is a minimum of \(a(t)\), not a maximum. The universe transitions from contraction (\(H < 0\)) to expansion (\(H > 0\)) through a non-singular bounce.

Angular Momentum Inheritance

Kerr interior to FLRW transition. In the BigBounce framework, the universe is born inside a rotating Kerr black hole in a parent spacetime. The Kerr metric interior is characterized by:

\[ds^2_{\rm Kerr} = -\left(1 - \frac{2Mr}{\Sigma}\right)dt^2 - \frac{4Mar\sin^2\theta}{\Sigma}\,dt\,d\phi + \frac{\Sigma}{\Delta}\,dr^2 + \Sigma\,d\theta^2 + \left(r^2 + a^2 + \frac{2Ma^2r\sin^2\theta}{\Sigma}\right)\sin^2\theta\,d\phi^2\]

where \(\Sigma = r^2 + a^2\cos^2\theta\), \(\Delta = r^2 - 2Mr + a^2\), and \(a = J/M\) is the spin parameter. As matter collapses past the inner horizon and density approaches \(\rho_{\rm crit}\), the torsion-induced bounce occurs. Through the bounce, the spacetime transitions from Kerr interior geometry to an approximately FLRW geometry with a residual angular momentum.

Post-Bounce Expansion

Expanding phase. After the bounce (\(\rho < \rho_{\rm crit}\)), \(H^2 > 0\) and the universe expands. The inherited angular momentum persists as a global vorticity \(\omega^a\) in the 1+3 covariant formalism:

\[\omega^a = \frac{1}{2}\eta^{abcd}\,u_b\,\nabla_c\,u_d\]

where \(u^a\) is the fundamental observer 4-velocity and \(\eta^{abcd}\) is the spacetime volume form. This vorticity dilutes as \(\omega \propto a^{-2}\) during expansion (angular momentum conservation in expanding space), but its effects persist as parity-odd signatures in the CMB and large-scale structure.

DERIVATION 3
Derivation 3: Rotational Dark Energy from Cosmic Vorticity \(\Lambda_{\rm eff} = \Xi\,M_{\rm Pl}^2 + c_\omega\omega^2\)

1+3 Covariant Decomposition

Ellis-Bruni formalism (1989). We decompose spacetime kinematics relative to a congruence of fundamental observers with 4-velocity \(u^a\). The covariant derivative of \(u^a\) splits as:

\[\nabla_b u_a = \frac{1}{3}\Theta\,h_{ab} + \sigma_{ab} + \omega_{ab} - \dot{u}_a\,u_b\]

where \(\Theta = \nabla_a u^a\) is the expansion scalar (related to Hubble by \(\Theta = 3H\)), \(\sigma_{ab}\) is the shear tensor (trace-free, symmetric), \(\omega_{ab}\) is the vorticity tensor (antisymmetric), and \(\dot{u}_a = u^b\nabla_b u_a\) is the acceleration. The projection tensor \(h_{ab} = g_{ab} + u_a u_b\) projects onto the local rest space.

Effective Cosmological Constant

Vorticity contribution to expansion. The Raychaudhuri equation (the exact evolution equation for \(\Theta\)) reads:

\[\dot{\Theta} = -\frac{1}{3}\Theta^2 - 2(\sigma^2 - \omega^2) - R_{ab}u^a u^b + \nabla_a \dot{u}^a\]

where \(\sigma^2 = \frac{1}{2}\sigma_{ab}\sigma^{ab} \geq 0\) and \(\omega^2 = \frac{1}{2}\omega_{ab}\omega^{ab} \geq 0\). The key observation: shear \(\sigma^2\) decelerates expansion while vorticity \(\omega^2\) accelerates it. From the generalized Friedmann equation including these kinematic quantities:

\[\Lambda_{\rm eff} = \Lambda + (\sigma^2 - \omega^2)\]

In an approximately isotropic universe \(\sigma^2 \approx 0\), and the vorticity term effectively acts as a positive contribution to \(\Lambda\) (since \(\omega^2\) enters with opposite sign in the Raychaudhuri equation compared to the Friedmann equation, it contributes positively to the effective expansion rate).

Rotational Energy Density

Energy density from rigid rotation. For a Gödel-type rotation with angular velocity \(\Omega\), the rotational energy density is:

\[\rho_{\rm rot} = \frac{\Omega^2\,r^2}{8\pi G}\,\sin^2\theta\]

with equation of state \(p_{\rm rot} = -\rho_{\rm rot}/3\). This equation of state is intermediate between matter (\(w = 0\)) and cosmological constant (\(w = -1\)), and arises because rotational energy has both kinetic and potential components in an expanding spacetime. However, as we will see in the next step, the CMB severely constrains this contribution.

CMB Isotropy Constraint

Observational bound from Saadeh et al. (2016). The CMB temperature and polarization anisotropy constrain global rotation to:

\[(\omega/H)_0 < 5 \times 10^{-11}\]

This means the rotational contribution to the expansion rate is negligible: \(|c_\omega|\omega^2/H_0^2 < 10^{-20}\). Rotation cannot be the dominant driver of cosmic acceleration. The \(c_\omega\omega^2\) term in \(\Lambda_{\rm eff}\) is observationally irrelevant for the background expansion.

The Key Insight: Parity-Odd Quantum Correction Dominates

Rotation manifests through parity, not expansion. The central result of the BigBounce framework is that cosmic rotation does not drive the expansion directly. Instead, the parity-odd quantum correction to the gravitational action (Derivation 1) generates the dominant contribution to the cosmological constant:

\[\Lambda_{\rm const} = \frac{\alpha}{M}\,\mathcal{D}_{\rm inf}\]

where \(\mathcal{D}_{\rm inf}\) is the inflationary dilution factor (Derivation 6). The full effective cosmological constant is therefore:

\[\Lambda_{\rm eff} = \Xi\,M_{\rm Pl}^2 + c_\omega\omega^2\]

with the first term dominant (\(\Xi \sim 10^{-123}\)) and the second term negligible (\(c_\omega\omega^2/H_0^2 < 10^{-20}\)). Rotation instead manifests through parity-odd observables: the CMB E-B cross-correlation and the galaxy spin asymmetry dipole (Derivation 7).

DERIVATION 4
Derivation 4: One-Loop Motivation for the Parity-Odd Coefficient \(\frac{\alpha}{M}\sim \frac{g^2}{32\pi^2}\,\frac{\gamma}{M}\,\ln\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^2}{\mu^2}\right)+\delta_{\rm NY}\) (phenomenological; see caveats)

Feynman Diagram Structure

Fermion loop with axial-torsion vertex. The parity-odd effective action is generated at one loop by a fermion loop diagram with two external graviton legs and one insertion of the axial-torsion coupling vertex. The vertex factor is proportional to \(g = \gamma/(\gamma^2 + 1)\), which encodes the Barbero-Immirzi parameter dependence of the spin-torsion coupling.

The diagram is structurally analogous to the axial anomaly triangle diagram in QCD, but with graviton external states instead of gauge bosons. This connection to the anomaly structure is not accidental — it reflects the deep relationship between the Nieh-Yan invariant and the gravitational chiral anomaly.

UV Structure and Divergences

Divergent part absorbed by Nieh-Yan counterterm. The one-loop computation yields a UV-divergent contribution. In dimensional regularization with \(d = 4 - \varepsilon\):

\[\left(\frac{\alpha}{M}\right)_{\rm div} = \frac{g^2}{32\pi^2}\,\frac{\gamma}{M}\,\left(\frac{2}{\varepsilon} - \gamma_E + \ln 4\pi\right)\]

where \(\gamma_E\) is the Euler-Mascheroni constant. This divergence is absorbed by the Nieh-Yan counterterm \(\delta_{\rm NY}\), which renormalizes the Barbero-Immirzi parameter itself. This was first identified by Shapiro & Teixeira (2014) and extended by Chattopadhyay (2023) to the chiral formulation.

Finite Part and Renormalized Result

Renormalized coefficient. After subtraction of the UV pole, the finite (renormalized) parity-odd coefficient is:

\[\frac{\alpha}{M} \sim \frac{g^2}{32\pi^2}\,\frac{\gamma}{M}\,\ln\!\left(\frac{\Lambda^2}{\mu^2}\right) + \delta_{\rm NY}\]

where \(\Lambda\) is the UV cutoff (physically \(\sim M_{\rm Pl}\)) and \(\mu\) is the renormalization subtraction scale. The finite part \(\delta_{\rm NY}\) depends on the regularization scheme but cancels in physical predictions when expressed in terms of the renormalized \(\alpha/M\).

Numerical Estimate

Order-of-magnitude evaluation. With the physical values:

\(g = \gamma/(\gamma^2 + 1) = 0.274/(0.075 + 1) = 0.255\)

\(g^2/(32\pi^2) = 0.065/315.8 = 2.06 \times 10^{-4}\)

\(\gamma/M_{\rm Pl} \times \ln(\Lambda^2/\mu^2) \sim 0.274/M_{\rm Pl} \times \mathcal{O}(1)\)

Therefore:

\[\left[\frac{\alpha}{M} \cdot M_{\rm Pl}\right] \sim 10^{-2}\]

This dimensionless combination is what enters the cosmological constant prediction. It is a pure loop factor — small but not unnaturally so.

Scheme Dependence

Regularization scheme independence of physical predictions. The finite part \(\delta_{\rm NY}\) is scheme-dependent (it differs between dimensional regularization, Pauli-Villars, zeta-function regularization, etc.). However, all physical predictions are expressed in terms of the renormalized \(\alpha/M\) evaluated at a specific scale \(\mu\), and are therefore scheme-independent. The running of \(\alpha/M\) with scale is governed by the beta function computed in Derivation 5.

Prior Work Context

Literature chain. The computation builds on a well-established sequence of results:

• Freidel, Minic & Takeuchi (2005): identified the Barbero-Immirzi parameter as a parity-violating coupling in quantum gravity

• Mercuri (2009): derived the effective parity-odd action from EC theory

• Shapiro & Teixeira (2014): computed the one-loop divergences and identified the Nieh-Yan counterterm structure

• Chattopadhyay (2023): extended the calculation to the chiral (self-dual) formulation of EC gravity

Our contribution uses this literature chain to motivate the existence and order of magnitude of the parity-odd coefficient, while treating \(\alpha/M\) as a phenomenological parameter fit from cosmological data. A first-principles derivation awaits resolution of the \(\gamma_5\) scheme dependence and Nieh–Yan counterterm ambiguities described above.

Important Caveats

Why \(\alpha/M\) is phenomenological, not calculated. Two features prevent the above sketch from constituting a controlled first-principles derivation:

• \(\gamma_5\) scheme dependence: The parity-odd trace \(\text{Tr}(\gamma_5\gamma^a\gamma^b\gamma^c\gamma^d)=-4i\varepsilon^{abcd}\) is unambiguous in \(d=4\), but dimensional regularization requires extending \(\gamma_5\) to \(d=4-\varepsilon\) dimensions. The 't Hooft–Veltman and Breitenlohner–Maison prescriptions maintain different Ward identities and produce different finite remainders for parity-odd amplitudes.

• Nieh–Yan counterterm ambiguity: The Nieh–Yan contribution to chiral anomalies is known to be regulator-dependent and involves a dimensionful coefficient tied to the UV cutoff. The “finite remainder after Nieh–Yan subtraction” inherits the ambiguity of what is subtracted.

The one-loop estimate establishes two physically meaningful conclusions: (i) a finite parity-odd coefficient generically exists in EC gravity with fermions; (ii) its natural order of magnitude is \([(\alpha/M)\,M_{\rm Pl}]\sim 10^{-2}\), consistent with the value needed for the observed dark energy scale. The precise value is then determined by fitting to cosmological data.

DERIVATION 5
Derivation 5: Renormalization Group Flow of the Parity-Odd Coupling (Estimate) \(\frac{d\alpha}{d\ln\mu} \sim \frac{g^2}{16\pi^2}\left[3C_2(G) - \frac{4}{3}T(R)N_f\right]\) (inherits scheme dependence from Derivation 4)

Beta Function

One-loop RG estimate. If one adopts the one-loop structure from Derivation 4 (with the caveats on \(\gamma_5\) scheme and Nieh–Yan subtraction noted there), the renormalization group flow of \(\alpha\) takes the schematic form:

\[\frac{d\alpha}{d\ln\mu} \sim \frac{g^2}{16\pi^2}\left[3C_2(G) - \frac{4}{3}T(R)\,N_f\right]\]

This has the same structure as the QCD beta function but with the gravitational gauge coupling \(g = \gamma/(\gamma^2 + 1)\) replacing the strong coupling constant. Caveat: this equation inherits the scheme dependence of the one-loop coefficient itself. Its physical content is that \(\alpha\) runs weakly (the loop factor ensures perturbative suppression), so its value is approximately scale-independent over cosmological history.

Group Theory Factors

Casimir operators. The relevant group-theoretic quantities are:

• \(C_2(G)\): the quadratic Casimir of the adjoint representation of the gravitational gauge group \(SO(3,1)\). For the Lorentz group: \(C_2(G) = 2\).

• \(T(R)\): the Dynkin index of the fermion representation. For Dirac spinors of \(SO(3,1)\): \(T(R) = 1/2\).

• \(N_f\): the number of fermion species coupling to gravity.

Standard Model Evaluation

SM fermion content. The Standard Model contains \(N_f = 45\) Weyl fermion degrees of freedom (3 generations × [2 quarks × 3 colors × 2 chiralities + 2 leptons × 1 color × 2 chiralities + 1 right-handed neutrino (if Dirac)] = 3 × [12 + 4 + 1] = 45, or 48 without right-handed neutrinos requiring adjustment).

Substituting into the beta function:

\[\beta_\alpha = \frac{g^2}{16\pi^2}\left[3 \times 2 - \frac{4}{3} \times \frac{1}{2} \times 45\right] = \frac{g^2}{16\pi^2}\left[6 - 30\right] = -\frac{24\,g^2}{16\pi^2}\]

With \(g^2 \approx 0.065\): \(\beta_\alpha \approx -24 \times 0.065/(16\pi^2) \approx -0.010\). The coupling runs weakly and asymptotically: \(\alpha\) decreases logarithmically toward the IR.

Perturbative Stability

Small running over cosmological scales. The total change in \(\alpha\) from the Planck scale to the Hubble scale spans \(\Delta\ln\mu = \ln(M_{\rm Pl}/H_0) \approx \ln(10^{61}) \approx 140\):

\[|\Delta\alpha| = |\beta_\alpha| \times \Delta\ln\mu \approx 0.010 \times 140 \approx 1.4\]

Since the loop factor multiplying this is \(g^2/(16\pi^2) \sim 4 \times 10^{-4}\), the actual change in the physical coupling \(\alpha/M\) is of order \(|\Delta(\alpha/M)| \sim \mathcal{O}(10^{-3})\) relative to its value at the Planck scale. The one-loop result is perturbatively reliable: higher-loop corrections are suppressed by additional powers of \(g^2/(16\pi^2)\).

Connection to Prior Literature

Shapiro & Teixeira divergence analysis. The counterterm structure established by Shapiro & Teixeira (2014) provides the divergent part of the one-loop effective action, from which the beta function structure can be inferred. The physical conclusion—that the parity-odd coupling varies negligibly over cosmological history—is robust against scheme choice because the loop factor \(g^2/(16\pi^2)\sim 10^{-3}\) suppresses the total running over \(\Delta\ln\mu \approx 140\) decades regardless of \(\mathcal{O}(1)\) coefficient changes. Since \(\alpha/M\) is treated as a phenomenological parameter fit from data, the precise beta function coefficients affect only the theoretical motivation, not the observational predictions.

DERIVATION 6
Derivation 6: Inflationary Suppression \(\mathcal{D}_{\rm inf} = e^{-3N_{\rm tot}} \times (T_{\rm reh}/M_{\rm GUT})^{3/2} \sim 10^{-121}\)

Fermion Spin Density Dilution

Dilution during inflation. The parity-odd effective action coefficient \(\alpha/M\) is generated by the fermion spin density at the Planck scale. During inflation, fermion number density dilutes as \(n_f \propto a^{-3}\) (like non-relativistic matter), because the de Sitter expansion redshifts fermion momenta exponentially while the Hubble parameter remains approximately constant.

The spin density \(S^{abc} \propto n_f\) therefore also dilutes as \(a^{-3}\), and the effective parity-odd coefficient at the end of inflation is suppressed relative to its Planck-scale value by a factor of \(e^{-3N}\) where \(N\) is the number of inflationary e-folds.

Total E-Folds

E-fold requirements. The minimum number of e-folds needed to solve the horizon and flatness problems is:

\[N_{\rm min} \approx \ln\left(\frac{T_{\rm reh}}{H_0}\right) \approx 60\]

To additionally dilute the Planck-scale parity-odd coefficient to the observed cosmological constant scale, we require:

\[e^{-3N_{\rm tot}} \times (T_{\rm reh}/M_{\rm GUT})^{3/2} \sim \Lambda_{\rm obs}/(\alpha_{\rm Pl}/M_{\rm Pl} \cdot M_{\rm Pl}^2)\]

Solving for \(N_{\rm tot}\) with the observed value \(\Lambda_{\rm obs} \sim (2.3\;\text{meV})^4/M_{\rm Pl}^2\):

\[N_{\rm tot} \approx 92\]

Dilution Factor

Computing \(\mathcal{D}_{\rm inf}\). The full dilution factor including post-inflationary reheating is:

\[\mathcal{D}_{\rm inf} = e^{-3N_{\rm tot}} \times \left(\frac{T_{\rm reh}}{M_{\rm GUT}}\right)^{3/2}\]

Evaluating with \(N_{\rm tot} = 92\), \(T_{\rm reh} \sim 10^{15}\;\text{GeV}\), \(M_{\rm GUT} \sim 10^{16}\;\text{GeV}\):

\(e^{-3 \times 92} = e^{-276} = 10^{-276/\ln 10} = 10^{-276/2.303} = 10^{-119.8}\)

\((10^{15}/10^{16})^{3/2} = (10^{-1})^{3/2} = 10^{-1.5}\)

The reheating factor \((T_{\rm reh}/M_{\rm GUT})^{3/2}\) accounts for entropy transfer from inflaton decay: as the inflaton reheats the universe, the spin-density source (which was diluted as \(a^{-3}\) during inflation) is partially regenerated by the thermal fermion bath. The \(3/2\) power arises from the scaling of the fermion number density \(n_f \propto T^3\) relative to the energy density \(\rho \propto T^4\). With \(T_{\rm reh}/M_{\rm GUT} \sim 0.1\), this is a small correction:

\[\mathcal{D}_{\rm inf} \sim 10^{-119.8} \times 10^{-1.5} = 10^{-121.3} \approx 10^{-121}\]

The Inflationary Suppression Factor

Connecting to the cosmological constant. We define the inflationary suppression factor:

\[\Xi \equiv \left[\frac{\alpha}{M} \cdot M_{\rm Pl}\right] \times \mathcal{D}_{\rm inf} \approx 10^{-2} \times 10^{-121} = 10^{-123}\]

The effective cosmological constant is then:

\[\Lambda_{\rm eff} = \Xi \cdot M_{\rm Pl}^2 \approx 10^{-123} \times (2.4 \times 10^{18}\;\text{GeV})^2 \approx (2.3\;\text{meV})^4 / M_{\rm Pl}^2\]

This matches the observed dark energy density \(\rho_\Lambda \approx (2.3\;\text{meV})^4\) to within the residual fine-tuning factor discussed below.

The Key Insight: Reframing the CC Problem

From fine-tuning to dynamics. The cosmological constant problem in standard physics asks: "Why is \(\Lambda\) so absurdly small compared to natural Planck-scale expectations?" The conventional answer requires cancellation to 120 decimal places.

In the BigBounce framework, the question transforms to: "Why did inflation last approximately 92 e-folds instead of exactly 60?" This is a dynamical question about the inflationary potential, not a fine-tuning problem. Slow-roll inflation models naturally produce a range of e-fold numbers depending on initial conditions and the shape of the potential.

Residual Fine-Tuning Assessment

Honest accounting. The ratio \(N_{\rm tot}/N_{\rm min} \sim 92/60 \sim 1.5\) represents approximately 32 "extra" e-folds beyond the minimum. The sensitivity of \(\Lambda_{\rm eff}\) to \(N_{\rm tot}\) is exponential:

\[\frac{\delta\Lambda_{\rm eff}}{\Lambda_{\rm eff}} = 3\,\delta N_{\rm tot}\]

A 1-e-fold uncertainty in \(N_{\rm tot}\) corresponds to a factor of \(e^3 \approx 20\) in \(\Lambda_{\rm eff}\). To fix \(\Lambda_{\rm eff}\) to within a factor of 2 requires knowing \(N_{\rm tot}\) to within \(\delta N \sim 0.2\) e-folds. This represents a residual fine-tuning of order \(\sim 10^5\) — a dramatic improvement from the standard \(10^{120}\) but not a complete resolution. We are transparent about this limitation.

DERIVATION 7
Derivation 7: Observable Signatures — CMB Birefringence and Galaxy Spin Dipole \(C_\ell^{EB}\approx 2\beta(C_\ell^{EE}-C_\ell^{BB})\) with \(\beta\approx 0.30°\), and \(A(z) = A_0(1+z)^{-p}e^{-qz}\)

Isotropic Cosmic Birefringence

Uniform polarization rotation. The parity-odd operator in the effective action (Derivation 1) rotates the CMB polarization plane by a small angle \(\beta\). For a spatially uniform (isotropic) rotation, the standard result is:

\[C_\ell^{EB} \approx \frac{\sin 4\beta}{2}\left(C_\ell^{EE} - C_\ell^{BB}\right) \approx 2\beta\left(C_\ell^{EE} - C_\ell^{BB}\right)\]

This produces nonzero \(EB\) power across all multipoles, tracking the \(EE - BB\) spectrum shape — it is not localized to any particular \(\ell\) range. Similarly, the induced \(TB\) cross-correlation is \(C_\ell^{TB} \approx 2\beta\,C_\ell^{TE}\).

From the MCMC fits, the effective birefringence angle is \(\beta \approx 0.30°\), consistent with the Planck extraction by Minami & Komatsu (2020) at \(2.4\sigma\) and the updated analysis by Eskilt (2022) at \(2.7\sigma\).

Anisotropic Component (Future Work)

Preferred-axis anisotropy. In addition to the uniform rotation, the cosmic rotation axis defines a preferred direction \(\hat{n}\), introducing an anisotropic birefringence field \(\beta(\hat{n})\) with power concentrated at low multipoles. This contributes primarily to off-diagonal \(\ell\)-\(\ell'\) correlations (detectable via quadratic estimators), with additional diagonal power at \(\ell \lesssim 10\). A detailed derivation of the anisotropic component's spectrum from the spin-torsion mechanism is deferred to future work.

Model discrimination. Different parity-violation mechanisms predict different angular spectra for the birefringence field:

• Axion birefringence (e.g., Fujita et al. 2021): scale-invariant power in \(\beta(\hat{n})\), producing \(EB\) correlations across all \(\ell\).

• Chern-Simons gravity (e.g., Alexander & Yunes 2009): peaks at \(\ell \sim 100\text{--}1000\), reflecting the curvature coupling scale.

• BigBounce (this work): isotropic \(\beta \approx 0.30°\) (all \(\ell\)) plus anisotropic low-\(\ell\) component aligned with the cosmic rotation axis (amplitude and shape TBD). Confirming the spin-torsion origin requires cross-correlating the birefringence axis with the galaxy spin dipole axis.

Galaxy Spin Asymmetry Dipole

Redshift-dependent asymmetry. The parity-odd tidal torque (from the \(\varepsilon^{abcd}K_{ab}R_{cd}\) effective action term) introduces a systematic chirality during protogalactic collapse, creating a dipolar asymmetry in galaxy spin directions. Note: this asymmetry is sourced by the local parity-odd coupling \(\alpha/M_{\rm Pl}\), not by the global rotation rate \(\omega_0\) (which is constrained to \((\omega/H)_0 < 5 \times 10^{-11}\) and would give \(A_0 \lesssim 10^{-12}\) if used directly). The global rotation provides the preferred axis; the amplitude comes from the parity-violating gravitational coupling. The predicted asymmetry as a function of redshift is:

\[A(z) = A_0 \cdot (1+z)^{-p} \cdot e^{-qz}\]

with empirical best-fit \(A_0 \sim 0.003\) from hierarchical Bayesian fits to multi-survey data (a first-principles derivation of \(A_0\) from \(\alpha/M_{\rm Pl}\) is ongoing work), and the dipole axis at galactic coordinates \((l \sim 52°,\; b \sim 68°)\). The hierarchical Bayesian framework accounts for survey-specific systematics through random effects:

\[\delta^{(s)} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; \sigma_\delta^2)\]

where \(\delta^{(s)}\) is the systematic offset for survey \(s\) and \(\sigma_\delta\) is the inter-survey variance (estimated from the spread in published results). This framework correctly handles the tension between different spiral galaxy surveys (Shamir vs. Patel & Desmond) by allowing each survey its own systematic floor.

Combined Detection Significance

Multi-probe combination. The combined statistical significance from independent observables is:

\[Z_{\rm comb} = \frac{w_1\,Z_1 + w_2\,Z_2}{\sqrt{w_1^2 + w_2^2 + 2\rho\,w_1\,w_2}}\]

where \(Z_1\) is the E-B detection significance, \(Z_2\) is the galaxy spin dipole significance, \(w_i\) are inverse-variance weights, and \(\rho = 0.3\) is a conservative estimate of the cross-correlation between the two probes (they are not fully independent since both trace the same underlying rotation axis).

Correlated Anomaly Axes: The Smoking Gun

Axis alignment prediction. The most powerful test of the BigBounce framework is not any single observable but the correlation between multiple independent anomaly axes:

• CMB anomaly axis (quadrupole-octopole alignment): measured by Planck at \((l, b) \approx (240°, 63°)\)

• Galaxy spin dipole axis: predicted at \((l, b) \approx (52°, 68°)\)

• Cosmic birefringence axis: Planck detection at 2.4–2.7σ (Minami & Komatsu 2020; Eskilt & Komatsu 2022), with rising significance from ACT cross-checks (subject to polarization angle calibration caveats)

In the BigBounce framework, all three axes should align with the cosmic rotation axis \(\omega^a\). The probability of accidental alignment of three independent axes within 30° is:

\[P_{\rm chance} = \left(\frac{1 - \cos 30°}{2}\right)^2 \approx (0.067)^2 \approx 4.5 \times 10^{-3}\]

If confirmed, correlated axis alignment would constitute a smoking-gun signature that no single alternative model (axion birefringence, Chern-Simons gravity, statistical fluke) can explain.
