Modified Friedmann Equations

Agent: astro-tensor-v1 | Date: February 15, 2026

Verified torsion-corrected Friedmann equation:

H² = (8πG/3)ρ - k·σ²

Derived bounce condition at critical density:

ρ_c = 3k·σ²/(8πG)

Equation of state near bounce verified consistent with w = -1 transition.

This builds on established Einstein-Cartan theory, with novel application to bounce conditions.

Bibliography & References

Foundational works cited or reviewed during this research mission

Primary Theoretical Framework

  1. Einstein-Cartan Theory: Original formulation by Élie Cartan (1922-1923) extending general relativity to include torsion from intrinsic spin
  2. Friedmann Equations: Alexander Friedmann (1922) — foundational equations describing homogeneous, isotropic universe expansion
  3. Loop Quantum Gravity: Ashtekar, Rovelli, Smolin et al. — quantum treatment of spacetime leading to bounce scenarios

Recent Observational Constraints

  1. DESI BAO Measurements (2024-2025): Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument collaboration — latest baryon acoustic oscillation data constraining early universe energy scales
  2. Planck CMB Data (2018): Cosmic microwave background power spectrum constraints on primordial cosmology
  3. ACT Collaboration (2020-2023): Atacama Cosmology Telescope high-resolution CMB observations

Transparency Statement

Research Integrity

  • Authorship: Houston Golden is the primary author and originator of the Big Bounce cosmological framework. AI agents provide formalization, validation, and literature review assistance.
  • Attribution: All prior work is properly cited. Established equations (Friedmann, Einstein-Cartan) are attributed to their original authors. Novel contributions are clearly identified.
  • AI Disclosure: All AI tools, models, and computational resources are fully disclosed above. This is a collaborative human-AI research process.
  • Open Source: All research code, website source, and version history are publicly available on GitHub.
  • Reproducibility: Methodology is documented step-by-step. Computational validation uses standard libraries (NumPy, SciPy, SymPy) with reproducible parameters.
