Mission Overview

Research Question: Can a Big Bounce cosmological model with quantum gravity corrections produce testable predictions consistent with current observational data, and is the paper presenting this model rigorous enough for arXiv submission?

Houston Golden has been developing these theoretical ideas about cosmology and white holes for years, originally inspired by studies at Cambridge. The paper presents a framework connecting quantum gravity corrections to observable cosmological signatures. AI research assistants help formalize, validate, and strengthen what Houston has been working on — but the core ideas, theories, and creative insights are his own.

Research Methodology

This mission follows a five-phase methodology designed to transform Houston's theoretical framework into an arXiv-ready manuscript:

  1. Literature Review (Phase 1): Atlas conducts comprehensive surveys of the cosmological literature, mapping prior Big Bounce work, quantum gravity approaches, and white hole cosmology to identify knowledge gaps and ensure proper citation of existing frameworks.
  2. Mathematical Validation (Phase 2): Tensor rigorously verifies all equations, derivations, and mathematical claims. Runs numerical stability checks and validates consistency of the quantum gravity corrections with established physics.
  3. Data Analysis (Phase 3): Nova explores alternative interpretations of observational data, while Tensor compares model predictions against CMB data, large-scale structure observations, and other cosmological constraints.
  4. Paper Synthesis (Phase 4): Sage integrates all validated findings, properly cited literature, and confirmed predictions into a coherent, well-structured manuscript following arXiv standards.
  5. Peer Review Simulation (Phase 5): Keane conducts rigorous simulated peer review, identifying potential weaknesses, unclear arguments, missing citations, and areas requiring strengthening before submission.

Each phase builds on the previous, ensuring the final paper is mathematically sound, properly contextualized in existing literature, and ready for scrutiny by the astrophysics community.
