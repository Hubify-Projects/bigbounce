1. Introduction

The initial singularity of classical Big Bang cosmology remains one of the most significant challenges in theoretical physics. Quantum gravity effects, negligible at low energies, are expected to become dominant near the Planck scale, potentially resolving the singularity through a "Big Bounce" — a smooth transition from a contracting to an expanding phase.

Multiple theoretical frameworks have been proposed to realize bounce cosmology, including Loop Quantum Cosmology (LQC), string gas cosmology, and modifications of general relativity incorporating torsion. This research investigates bounce dynamics within the Einstein-Cartan (EC) framework, where spacetime torsion couples to intrinsic spin angular momentum of matter fields.

Our central research question asks: Can a Big Bounce cosmological model with quantum gravity corrections produce testable predictions consistent with current observational data, and is the paper presenting this model rigorous enough for arXiv submission? This question motivates a systematic investigation spanning literature review, mathematical validation, data comparison, manuscript synthesis, and peer review simulation.

2. Prior Work and Theoretical Context

2.1 Einstein-Cartan Theory

The Einstein-Cartan theory was developed independently by Élie Cartan (1922-1923) and extended by Dennis Sciama (1962) and Tom Kibble (1961). The theory generalizes Einstein's general relativity by allowing spacetime to possess both curvature and torsion. All equations from EC theory used in this work are standard results from this established framework — our contribution lies in their application to bounce cosmology and comparison with recent observational constraints.

The key modification to the Einstein field equations introduces a torsion tensor \(S^\lambda_{\mu\nu}\) coupled to spin density. The resulting field equations (Hehl et al., 1976; Trautman, 2006) include additional terms that become significant at high densities.

2.2 Loop Quantum Cosmology Approaches

Loop Quantum Cosmology (Bojowald, 2001; Ashtekar & Singh, 2011) provides an alternative quantum gravity framework that naturally predicts a bounce through discrete quantum geometry effects. LQC replaces the classical singularity with a quantum bridge, with the bounce occurring when the matter density reaches approximately \(0.41\rho_{\text{Planck}}\).

2.3 String Gas Cosmology

String gas cosmology (Brandenberger & Vafa, 1989; Battefeld & Watson, 2006) offers a distinct mechanism for bounce realization through string-theoretic effects, including T-duality and winding modes. While conceptually different from EC theory, string gas models share the feature of singularity resolution through quantum corrections.

2.4 Recent Observational Constraints (2025-2026)

The Dark Energy Spectroscopic Instrument (DESI) collaboration released updated Baryon Acoustic Oscillation (BAO) measurements in 2024-2025, providing the most stringent constraints to date on early universe dynamics and bounce energy scales. These measurements, combined with Planck CMB data and large-scale structure surveys, enable unprecedented tests of bounce cosmology predictions.

4. Mathematical Framework

4.1 Torsion-Corrected Friedmann Equations

Attribution: The standard Friedmann equations derive from Einstein's field equations (Friedmann, 1922, 1924). The torsion corrections we employ follow from the Einstein-Cartan extension developed by Cartan, Sciama, and Kibble (see Section 2.1). Our contribution is the specific application of these corrections to bounce cosmology and the derivation of explicit bounce conditions from recent observational constraints.

Starting from the Einstein-Cartan field equations with torsion-spin coupling, we obtain the modified Friedmann equation for a spatially flat universe:

\[ H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - k\sigma^2 \]

where \(H = \dot{a}/a\) is the Hubble parameter, \(\rho\) is the energy density, \(k\) is a dimensionless coupling constant characterizing torsion-spin interactions, and \(\sigma\) represents the spin density tensor magnitude. This equation differs from the standard Friedmann equation through the additional term \(-k\sigma^2\), which becomes significant at high densities.

4.2 Bounce Condition Derivation

Novel contribution: While bounce conditions have been derived in various quantum gravity frameworks, our specific derivation of the critical density in terms of the torsion coupling parameter \(k\) and spin density \(\sigma\) provides new constraints that can be compared with observational data.

The bounce occurs when the scale factor \(a(t)\) reaches a minimum, corresponding to \(H = 0\). Setting the modified Friedmann equation to zero yields the critical density at bounce:

\[ \rho_c = \frac{3k\sigma^2}{8\pi G} \]

This critical density represents the maximum energy density achieved during the bounce phase. For physically reasonable values of the spin density near the Planck scale, this predicts \(\rho_c \sim 0.1\text{--}0.5 \, \rho_{\text{Planck}}\), comparable to Loop Quantum Cosmology predictions.

4.3 Equation of State Near Bounce

The effective equation of state parameter \(w = p/\rho\) (where \(p\) is pressure) characterizes the matter content driving the bounce. Analysis of the continuity equation with torsion corrections shows that:

\[ w_{\text{eff}}(t_{\text{bounce}}) \approx -1 + \mathcal{O}\left(\frac{\rho}{\rho_c}\right) \]

The approach to \(w \to -1\) near the bounce indicates that torsion-induced corrections effectively generate negative pressure, consistent with the requirement for a non-singular bounce. This specific functional form and its derivation from EC field equations represents our novel contribution, extending previous phenomenological bounce models.

6.2 Comparison with Alternative Bounce Mechanisms

Our torsion-based bounce mechanism differs from Loop Quantum Cosmology in that the bounce arises from classical modifications to general relativity (torsion) rather than quantum discreteness effects. The critical densities are comparable (\(\sim 0.1\text{--}0.5 \, \rho_{\text{Planck}}\)), but the dynamical evolution and observational signatures may differ.

Compared to string gas cosmology, our approach does not invoke extra dimensions or string-specific physics. This makes the model more directly testable with current cosmological observations, though potentially less unified from a fundamental physics perspective.
