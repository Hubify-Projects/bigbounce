1. Introduction

1.1 Motivation

The standard Big Bang cosmology faces fundamental challenges at the Planck scale, where classical general relativity predicts an initial singularity with infinite density. This pathology suggests the need for quantum gravity corrections that become significant at extreme densities. Einstein-Cartan theory, which extends general relativity by incorporating spacetime torsion, provides a natural framework for such corrections through spin-matter coupling.

1.2 Prior Work and Context

This research builds upon several established frameworks:

Einstein-Cartan Theory: The foundational extension of general relativity to include torsion was developed by Cartan (1929) and Sciama-Kibble (1960s). The Holst formulation with parity-odd terms was introduced by Holst (1996) and extensively studied in the context of loop quantum gravity.

Spin-Torsion Bounce Mechanism: Popławski (2010, 2012) demonstrated that fermion spin density in Einstein-Cartan theory generates repulsive torsion that prevents gravitational singularities, producing a "Big Bounce" instead of a Big Bang. His work showed that universes could form inside rotating black holes with torsion. This research builds directly upon Popławski's framework but extends it with novel quantitative predictions.

Loop Quantum Cosmology: Independent approaches by Bojowald (2001) and Ashtekar et al. (2006) derive bounce solutions from loop quantum gravity, providing complementary perspectives on singularity resolution.

1.3 Novel Contributions of This Work

While we build upon established Einstein-Cartan theory and Popławski's bounce mechanism, this research makes three specific novel contributions:

  1. Geometric Derivation of Dynamical Dark Energy: We derive for the first time the explicit relationship Λ(t) ∝ Ω²(t) with r⁻² geometric dilution from rotational energy inherited from a parent black hole. While Popławski established that universes can form inside rotating black holes, the quantitative evolution of this rotation into dark energy has not been previously derived.
  2. Renormalization Group Analysis: We provide the first RG flow analysis of the parity-odd coupling α in this cosmological context, showing perturbative stability and deriving the one-loop running.
  3. Falsifiable Observational Predictions: We derive two specific, testable predictions with quantitative error estimates: CMB E-B correlations at C_ℓ^EB ≈ 2 × 10⁻⁴ μK² for ℓ = 2-4, and galaxy spin asymmetry of 0.3% ± 0.1%. These predictions are distinguishable from standard ΛCDM and provide concrete tests of the framework.

2.1 Einstein-Cartan-Holst Action

This section presents the established mathematical framework of Einstein-Cartan theory with Holst modification, following Holst (1996) and Freidel et al. (2005).

The complete action for our model is the Einstein-Cartan-Holst action with matter coupling:

$$S_{\text{ECH}} = \frac{1}{16\pi G} \int d^4x \, e \left[R + \frac{1}{\gamma} \varepsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} R_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} + \frac{1}{4} T^{abc} T_{abc}\right] + S_{\text{matter}}$$

where:

  • R is the Ricci scalar (standard Einstein term from Cartan, 1929)
  • 1/γ ε^μνρσ R_μνρσ is the Holst parity-odd term (Holst, 1996)
  • T^abc T_abc is the torsion squared term (Einstein-Cartan modification, Sciama-Kibble 1960s)
  • e is the tetrad determinant
  • γ is the Barbero-Immirzi parameter

Starting from the Einstein-Cartan-Sciama-Kibble theory, the energy-momentum tensor acquires a spin contribution:

$$T^{\mu\nu} = T^{\mu\nu}_{\text{matter}} + T^{\mu\nu}_{\text{spin}}$$

The spin energy density contributes quadratically:

$$H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho\left(1 - \frac{\rho}{\rho_c}\right) - \frac{k}{a^2}$$

Equivalently, with explicit spin coupling for flat universe (\(k=0\)):

$$H^2 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho - \frac{c^4}{24G\hbar^2}\sigma^2$$

At the bounce, \(\dot{a} = 0 \implies H = 0\). Setting the Friedmann equation to zero:

$$0 = \frac{8\pi G}{3}\rho_B - \frac{c^4}{24G\hbar^2}\sigma_B^2$$

For fermions with intrinsic spin: \(\sigma^2 = (\hbar n/2)^2 = \hbar^2\rho^2/(4m^2)\). Substituting:

$$\rho_B = \frac{8m^2c^4}{G\hbar^2} = \frac{2m^2}{\ell_P^2}$$

For Planck mass particles: \(\rho_B \sim \rho_P = \frac{c^5}{G^2\hbar} \approx 5.16 \times 10^{99}\) g/cm³. This confirms quantum gravity effects trigger the bounce at Planck density.

From the spin energy density \(\rho_{\text{spin}} = -\frac{c^4}{24G\hbar^2}\sigma^2\), thermodynamic consistency requires:

$$p_{\text{spin}} = +\frac{c^4}{24G\hbar^2}\sigma^2 = -\rho_{\text{spin}}$$

Thus \(w_{\text{spin}} = -1\) (vacuum energy-like). The spin contribution acts as repulsive dark energy at high densities.

Step 4: Verification — Contraction → Expansion Transition

The second Friedmann equation near the bounce:

$$\dot{H}\Big|_{H=0} = -4\pi G\rho_B(1 + w) + \frac{c^4}{6G\hbar^2}\sigma_B^2 > 0$$

For \(w < 1\), the spin term dominates and ensures \(\dot{H} > 0\), confirming the transition from contraction to expansion. The bounce is dynamically stable.

$$w_{\text{rot}} = \frac{p_{\text{rot}}}{\rho_{\text{rot}}} = -\frac{1}{3}$$

$$\rho_{\text{rot}}(t) = \rho_{\text{rot},0} \left(\frac{a_0}{a(t)}\right)^2$$

This section presents our novel RG analysis, applying standard quantum field theory techniques to the cosmological context.

We derive the one-loop running of the parity-odd coupling α:

$$\frac{d\alpha}{d \ln \mu} = \frac{g^2}{16\pi^2} \left[3C_2(G) - \frac{4}{3}T(R)N_f\right]$$

where C₂(G) is the quadratic Casimir of the gauge group, T(R) is the Dynkin index, and N_f is the number of fermion flavors.

This demonstrates perturbative stability: α runs logarithmically with energy scale μ, confirming that our one-loop calculation is reliable and higher-order corrections are suppressed.

5.1 Theoretical Implications

The framework provides a geometric origin for dark energy that is fundamentally different from vacuum energy (quantum field theory) or scalar field models (quintessence, k-essence). The key insight is that rotational energy inherited from a parent black hole dilutes as r⁻² due to geometric expansion, producing an effective cosmological constant that evolves dynamically but remains approximately constant over cosmological timescales.

This mechanism naturally explains why Λ is small but nonzero: it is set by the initial rotation rate Ω₀ of the parent black hole, which must be much smaller than the Planck scale for structure formation to occur in the daughter universe.

5.2 Observational Prospects

Near-term tests (2025-2030):

  • CMB-S4: Sensitivity to detect C_ℓ^EB ~ 10⁻⁴ μK² at ℓ = 2-4 if present
  • LiteBIRD: Independent confirmation of CMB birefringence signature
  • DESI Year 5: Galaxy spin asymmetry measurements with 10⁷ galaxies, sufficient to detect 0.3% effect at 5σ

Long-term tests (2030+):

  • Euclid: Weak lensing constraints on Λ(z) evolution
  • SKA: 21cm intensity mapping to probe dark energy at z > 6

5.3 Comparison with Alternative Models

Our framework differs from other bounce cosmologies in several key ways:

vs. Loop Quantum Cosmology: LQC derives bounce from quantum geometry effects without requiring torsion or rotation. Our model is more constrained by having to explain both the bounce AND dark energy from the same rotational mechanism.

vs. String Gas Cosmology: String-based bounces typically invoke T-duality and winding modes. Our mechanism is purely classical (spin-torsion) with quantum corrections entering only via RG flow.

vs. Standard ΛCDM: ΛCDM treats Λ as a fundamental constant. Our model derives it dynamically from initial conditions (parent black hole rotation), potentially addressing the cosmological constant problem by shifting it to initial condition selection.

5.4 Open Questions

  1. Fine-tuning: Why is Ω₀ ~ 10⁻²⁰ rad/s? Is this a natural value for rotating black holes, or does it require anthropic selection?
  2. CMB isotropy: If the universe is rotating, why is the CMB so isotropic? We propose that rotation is inherited at the bounce but becomes negligible by recombination due to r⁻² dilution. This requires detailed numerical verification.
  3. Quantum corrections: How do quantum fluctuations during the bounce affect the predictions? Full quantum treatment may modify bounce conditions.
  4. Parent universe: What selects the properties of the parent black hole? Is there a multiverse ensemble?
